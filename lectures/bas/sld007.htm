Notes:

    The most fundamental form of interpersonal communication over the Internet is E-Mail. Because E-Mail is not instantaneous communication, it can function on low-speed unreliable networks quite effectively. Before the Internet, many users used a network called UUNET for E-Mail. UUNET did not depend on the network being up continuously. Periodically, each system would dial-up its neighbors and transfer any queued mail to the next system. Eventually mail would arrive at its destination, sometimes taking as long as a week.
    One the Internet, systems attempt to deliver E-Mail even when there are partial network outages. Since mail messages are relatively small, if a message cannot be immediately transferred to its designation, it is saved on the local system. After some time period (typically 1/2 hour) the transfer is tried again. This makes E-Mail one of the most reliable activities on the Internet.
    When E-Mail usage moved form UUNET to the Internet, the Mail was generally delivered directly to the final destination across the Internet.
    One observation is that E-Mail is not much use if you have to call someone to tell them to read their mail. However at some point people read E-Mail so often that it is the fastest way to contact them if they don’t pick up the phone. It is often faster than leaving voice mail or a message on an answering machine
